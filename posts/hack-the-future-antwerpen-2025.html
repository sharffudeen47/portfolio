Hack The Future Antwerpen 2025: What Pressure Teaches You

A reflection on teamwork, creative problem-solving, and what it feels like when you must build, break, and learn fast — inspired by my experience at Hack The Future (12 November 2025).

On November 12, I joined Hack The Future Antwerpen 2025 a high-energy hackathon powered by De Cronos Groep. Hack The Future brings together IT and creative students for realistic challenges where you collaborate under time pressure, make quick decisions, and learn directly from professionals. This edition took place in Antwerp and runs across 12–13 November 2025, which instantly sets the tone: you don’t have time to “overthink” everything — you have to think clearly, prioritize, and execute.

What made this experience special for me is how it tests more than technical skills. The real difficulty is staying aligned as a team: communicating fast, splitting work without losing consistency, and keeping quality high even when the clock is loud. Our team pushed hard, and I’m proud that we reached a 62% score and ranked 3rd out of 12 teams. That result wasn’t only about getting the “right” answers it was about staying calm, iterating quickly, and trusting each other’s strengths.

The day was full of hands-on problem-solving around themes like AI, cloud, and automation. In environments like this, you learn a practical truth: tools matter, but structure matters more. When a challenge is complex, teams that document assumptions, validate inputs, and keep a clean flow (even a simple checklist) move faster and make fewer mistakes. That mindset is extremely close to cybersecurity work: reducing uncertainty, proving what you claim, and making decisions based on evidence.

This hackathon also connected nicely with my learning journey at Arxus, where I gained real-world cloud engineering experience using Terraform and Microsoft Azure. Working with infrastructure as code gives you a deeper respect for repeatability: if you can’t rebuild something, you don’t truly control it. That lesson transfers perfectly into security and operations — reliability and clarity are what make speed safe.

Finally, a personal note: experiences like this become memorable because of people. A big thanks to my friend Tibo Eycken for making the experience both fun and meaningful, and to Arxus for the motivating environment that keeps pushing me toward stronger engineering habits. Hack The Future reminded me why I enjoy this field: it rewards curiosity, teamwork, and the ability to learn fast under real pressure.
