Security That Fits Care: Lessons from WZC De Foyer

What I learned when cybersecurity became something that had to work in a real elderly care organisation.

Before WZC De Foyer, I mostly saw cybersecurity as a technical routine: find issues, confirm them, write a report. But in an elderly care organisation, the meaning changes. The network supports care, staff workflows, and services that must stay stable. My first big lesson was simple: security only helps if it also protects continuity.

Environment
Multi-site network and VLANs
Focus
Visibility and safe testing
Outcome
Evidence and roadmap
The mindset shift

In a lab, an open port is just a finding. In a care environment, it might be a fragile device or something that daily work depends on. I learned to slow down and ask: what is this system used for, who depends on it, and what is the safest way to improve it?

A big part of my work was dealing with technical noise. Not ignoring it, but making it understandable. I took raw scans and enumeration results and turned them into clear actions: what is urgent, what can wait, and what needs a longer plan. That’s where I felt most useful: connecting security and infrastructure in a realistic way.

What I delivered

  • Internal visibility (safe and scoped): mapping systems and exposed services while being careful with sensitive devices.
  • Evidence and governance support: organizing findings so they become actions and documentation, not just files.
  • Asset baseline: supporting an asset-first approach so decisions start from what exists.
  • Security master plan (2025 to 2027): a practical roadmap with priorities that match real constraints.
  • Automation proof of concept: scan output to structured draft reporting using Google Gemini CLI, with a human review step.
How I worked
  • Clear scope: only what was approved.
  • Versioned outputs: consistent naming and timestamps.
  • Logging: so results can be explained and trusted.
  • Human validation: AI help is useful, but judgment matters.

One thing that surprised me is how often the goal is not perfect security, but steady improvement. The best wins were usually simple: better visibility, better baselines, small fixes, and clear documentation. These changes stay, and they build a stronger foundation over time.

Lessons I’m taking forward

  • Risk depends on context: technical severity is not the whole story.
  • Clear writing matters: people need impact and simple next steps.
  • Visibility matters: you can’t protect what you can’t see or name.
  • Stability is safety: in healthcare, uptime is part of protection.

This internship shaped how I want to work. I’m not only interested in finding issues. I’m interested in building security that teams can maintain: clear, documented, and realistic.
