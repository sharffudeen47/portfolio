Threat Intelligence: From Threats to Tactics

An eye-opening Howest session with insights from the Centre for Cybersecurity Belgium (CCB) — and a reminder that precision, context, and speed are what make defense effective.

I had the great opportunity to attend the Howest Hogeschool Tech&Meet session on “Threat Intelligence: From Threats to Tactics” (October 7th). With insights from the Centre for Cybersecurity Belgium (CCB), it genuinely felt like a masterclass in proactive cyber defense — especially for me as a cybersecurity student.

Focus
From headlines → operations
Core skills
Precision + context
Outcome
Faster, smarter decisions
The big message

Threat intelligence isn’t about consuming more information — it’s about filtering what matters, prioritizing correctly, and responding before attackers do.

1) Advanced threat hunting with fingerprinting

One of the most impressive parts was seeing how threat hunters go beyond simple domain names. We explored how tools like J4X (for X.509 certificate fingerprints) and J4S (for server connection fingerprints) can be used to pivot on indicators and uncover malicious infrastructure with much higher confidence.

Why it’s powerful

What stood out to me is how the unique ordering of fingerprints can help analysts distinguish threats with incredible precision — even when adversaries rotate domains or try to “blend in.”

2) EPSS for smarter prioritization

Another key lesson was how prioritization becomes much more honest when you combine scoring systems. CVSS can describe potential impact, but it doesn’t always reflect real-world exploitation speed. That’s where EPSS becomes extremely practical.

The example that hit hard: a vulnerability with a CVSS score of 7.2 might not look urgent at first — but if the same issue has an EPSS score of 96.7%, it’s a strong signal that it’s likely to be exploited quickly. That changes the timeline immediately: patch without delay.

My takeaway rule
  • CVSS = how bad it could be
  • EPSS = how soon it’s likely to be exploited
  • Operations = act based on exposure + business impact + speed

3) Proactive nation-state defense: CCB “Spear Warning”

The CCB’s “Spear Warning” initiative was easily one of the most inspiring parts. By actively scanning Belgian cyberspace and adopting a real “hacker mindset,” CCB proactively notifies organizations about vulnerable assets before attackers can exploit them — even through email and physical letters.

What I loved is that it’s not only theory: this proactive approach has already led to a measurable drop in unpatched systems. That’s what operational threat intelligence looks like when it’s done with responsibility.

What I’m taking forward

Overall, this session emphasized that precision, context, and speed are key in threat intelligence and defense. It was one of the most valuable sessions I’ve attended this semester, because it showed how threat intelligence is actually applied in real life — not just talked about.

Big thank-you to the speaker and organizers for such a practical and eye-opening evening.
