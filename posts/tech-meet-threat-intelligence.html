Threat Intelligence: From Threats to Tactics

Notes from a Howest session with the Centre for Cybersecurity Belgium. It was a good reminder that good defense is not only tools, it is picking the right signals and acting fast.

I attended the Howest Tech&Meet session “Threat Intelligence: From Threats to Tactics” on October 7. The speaker shared insights from the Centre for Cybersecurity Belgium (CCB). For me, it felt very practical. It was less about big headlines and more about how defenders actually work day to day.

The big message

Threat intelligence is not about collecting more data. It is about finding what matters, ranking it properly, and acting early.

1) Fingerprinting for threat hunting

One of the strongest parts was seeing how analysts go beyond domain names. We looked at tools like J4X for X.509 certificate fingerprints and J4S for server connection fingerprints. These help you pivot on more stable signals, especially when attackers keep changing domains.

Why it helps

Fingerprints can stay consistent even when infrastructure changes. That makes it easier to connect related systems with more confidence.

2) EPSS for better patch priority

The session also explained why CVSS alone is not enough for prioritization. CVSS tells you the potential impact, but not how likely it is to be exploited soon. That is where EPSS is useful.

The example that stuck with me: a vulnerability with CVSS 7.2 might look “medium” at first, but if EPSS is 96.7 percent, it is a strong sign it may be exploited quickly. That should push it higher in your patch plan.

A simple rule I took from this
  • CVSS tells you how bad it could be
  • EPSS tells you how likely it is to be exploited soon
  • Decide using exposure, business impact, and time

3) CCB Spear Warning

Another highlight was the CCB “Spear Warning” initiative. The idea is simple and powerful: help organisations by warning them when vulnerable assets are visible, before attackers use the same information. The fact that they also reach out directly, even by letter, shows that the goal is real improvement, not just reporting.

What I liked most is the mindset behind it. Threat intelligence becomes useful when it leads to action, and this is a clear example of that.

What I’m taking forward

This session reminded me that good defense comes from a few basics done well: clear signals, good context, and fast response. It was one of the most useful talks I attended because it showed how threat intelligence is applied in real operations.

Thanks to the speaker and the organisers for a practical and eye-opening evening.
