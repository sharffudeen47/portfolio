Cross-Platform App Development with Flutter

Notes and reflections from a meetup exploring the current state of “build once, deploy everywhere” focusing on performance, ecosystem depth, and what it takes to deliver real cross-platform experiences.

This Tech&Meet session explored how cross‑platform app development has evolved by 2025 and why Flutter remains one of the most powerful options for writing once and deploying across multiple platforms. The key takeaway was clear: cross‑platform development doesn’t automatically mean compromise if you understand the trade‑offs and design with intent.

What “cross-platform” really means

One codebase, multiple targets but not without careful design.

Flutter promises that you can write your app once in Dart and deploy it on Android, iOS, web, and even desktop. However, success isn’t just about compiling for multiple platforms; it’s about creating a seamless experience on each. This means crafting responsive layouts, ensuring smooth performance, and integrating native behaviors where needed.

Why Flutter stands out

The discussion highlighted several reasons Flutter continues to dominate the cross‑platform space:

  • Performance focus: Flutter aims for smooth rendering at 60–120 fps, with robust tools to detect performance issues early in development.
  • Hot reload: Real‑time code updates that preserve state make UI iteration incredibly fast.
  • UI flexibility: The widget‑based architecture allows developers to design beautiful, consistent interfaces with fine control over animations and visuals.

A valuable insight shared during the talk: if your Flutter app stutters, it’s usually a signal to optimize your code not a failure of Flutter itself. That mindset places accountability on engineering discipline rather than the framework.

Flutter fundamentals that matter

The speaker broke down Flutter’s core idea succinctly: everything is a widget. Widgets describe your UI and compose the app’s visual structure as a tree. Two key widget types shape most development:

  • Stateless widgets for UI elements that never change internally.
  • Stateful widgets for components that update based on state or user input.

Real projects quickly introduce the need for broader state management. The main lesson: choose a state management solution that fits your app’s complexity. You can’t scale production software by spreading setState everywhere.

Ecosystem: packages and platform channels

Flutter’s vast ecosystem is one of its strengths. Packages provide access to everything from cameras to file systems and advanced UI features. Yet, one important piece of advice was to always check platform coverage and license terms before including third‑party packages in production apps.

When functionality requires native code, Flutter bridges the gap using platform channels, allowing Dart to communicate with Kotlin, Swift, or other native layers. Cross‑platform doesn’t eliminate the need for native code it just changes when and why you need it.

Beyond mobile: Flutter’s expanding reach

The session also showcased how Flutter extends far beyond traditional apps. Projects like Flame bring 2D game development into Flutter’s ecosystem, while desktop and embedded targets expand its versatility. However, participants also noted that for SEO‑driven websites, Flutter web may not always be the best fit it depends on project goals.

Personal reflection

As a cybersecurity and IT student, I naturally approach new frameworks through the lens of maintainability, security, and real‑world operation. This session reinforced an important truth: good engineering practices matter more than the tools themselves. Flutter can save time and effort across platforms, but success depends on thoughtful architecture, clean dependencies, and awareness of native integration needs.

My next step is to develop a small end‑to‑end Flutter app from UI design and state management to package integration and final deployment and document where the experience shines and where extra care is needed. That’s how theory becomes skill.

Session format: Intro → Framework deep dive → Q&A + networking.

Speaker: Thijs Pirmez (Howest Cyber3Lab) mobile developer specialized in Flutter, with a strong interest in AI and native integration.
